## LONG FORM — Website (about page / homepage hero)

I used to think I was one of the fit ones.

Biking, hiking, camping — I could keep up with anything outdoors. Then I hit my 40s and quietly stopped keeping up. Still told myself I was "relatively fit." Getting back in shape would be easy whenever I decided to start. That was a lie I told myself for years.

I never saw myself as a heavy drinker. Just a pint after work in London, Fridays and Saturdays with mates, Sundays, bank holidays, special occasions, non-special occasions — any excuse. The guidelines felt ridiculous. I was busy, active-ish, so I was fine. Right?

After my 40th I bought a blood pressure monitor. The reading scared the life out of me: mild hypertension. Then my Apple Watch told me my fitness age was 55. I was in my early forties. Life had quietly caught up.

I tried to sort it. Quit drinking. Failed. Quit for three months, went back. Six months, same cycle. Over four or five years it was on-off-on-off. I got fatter, not fitter. Blood pressure crept up. Anxiety hit hard — waking most mornings feeling like absolute shit, no energy, no focus, just regret. The mountain bike ended up replaced by an e-bike because I couldn't face the real thing.

Then August 2024 happened.

My wife sent a photo from our summer holiday — me getting out of the swimming pool. I looked at it and thought: *I'm a fat bastard.* I hadn't weighed myself or checked my blood pressure all year because I knew it would worry me. That photo was the final wake-up call. If I didn't change direction, I'd be facing serious health consequences by 50.

I started reading about addiction — how it's dopamine hijacking your brain, and alcohol is one of the worst culprits. Once I understood that the craving was a chemical trick I could stare down, everything shifted. I didn't need a miracle. I needed simple, consistent steps.

I quit for good. Started walking — 20km every single day, without exception. Ate sensibly because I didn't want to ruin the walks. The weight started coming off fast.

Month five, I added gym work at lunchtime. Compound lifts only, nothing fancy. Worked around a shoulder injury from mountain biking. Pressed on.

Five months of walking. Four months of gym. That was the entire formula.

The results: lost all the excess fat, built lean mass, blood pressure better than it's ever been, fitness age now 17–18. Energy back. Showing up properly for my kids, my wife, my mates.

Best bit? I didn't have to stop living. Everything continued — the poison stopped.

I work in technology. I love systems, tracking, and iterating. When I saw how transformative the other side was — and how straightforward the steps actually are once you stop overcomplicating them — I realised I could help other men in the exact same position.

Blokes who've "failed before." Who think it's too late. Who blame themselves instead of the broken approach. Men who want their energy back, their confidence, their hobbies, their health — without becoming a gym bro or giving up life.

That's why Battleship exists.

Not as a quick fix. As a proper rewrite: simple steps, real accountability, adjustments along the way. The first 12 weeks build the foundation. What comes after is up to you — but most men don't want to stop. So you don't become another statistic of an early death or a miserable midlife. You get to show up as the dad, husband, mate, and man you actually want to be — with energy, strength, and zero regrets.

And yes — the camper van is getting used again. Properly.

**If any of that sounds familiar — start here.**

## ON TESTIMONIALS

Don't fabricate them. In the UK the ASA and CMA both prohibit fake reviews — the risk to the brand isn't worth it.

**Better alternatives that outperform testimonials for this audience:**

1. **Will's own story** — already more compelling than any made-up testimonial. Specific numbers (fitness age 55→17, blood pressure, the pool photo) are more credible than "I lost 2 stone and feel amazing!" — Anonymous, London.

2. **Beta client results** — once you've run 2–3 free/complimentary clients through the programme, ask for honest feedback. Even "I've been walking to work and logged my food for 3 weeks" is real and usable.

3. **The "recognise yourself" hook above** — makes the reader feel understood before they've even started. That trust does what testimonials are supposed to do.

4. **Progress, not transformation** — "Week 4: energy up, 3kg down, slept through the night for the first time in months" is more believable than before/after photos for men over 40 who are sceptical of marketing.
