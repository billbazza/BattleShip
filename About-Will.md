# About Will – Battleship – Midlife Fitness Reset

**William George Battleship Barratt**  
47 (48 this year) | Stubborn ex-drinker turned 17–18 fitness age at 47 | Founder of Battleship – Midlife Fitness Reset

I used to think I was one of the "fit ones."  
All my life I could bike for miles, hike hills, camp in the rain, keep up with anything outdoors. Hobbies? Rusty old camper vans and mountain biking — proper escapes from the daily grind.

Then I hit my 40s.  
I still told myself I was "relatively fit." Getting back in shape would be easy when I decided. Complete rubbish, as it turned out.

I never saw myself as a heavy drinker.  
Just liked a pint after work in London, Fridays and Saturdays with mates, Sundays, bank holidays, holidays, special occasions, non-special occasions… basically any excuse. The guidelines felt ridiculous — I was busy, active(ish), so I was fine. Right?

After my 40th, I bought a blood pressure monitor.  
The reading scared the life out of me: mild hypertension. Then an Apple Watch told me my fitness age was 55. Life had quietly caught up.

I tried to sort it.  
Quit drinking. Failed. Quit for three months, went back. Quit for six months, same cycle. Over four or five years it was on-off-on-off. I got fatter, not fitter. Blood pressure crept up. Mood sank. Anxiety hit hard — waking most mornings feeling like absolute shit, no energy, no focus, just regret. Hobbies? Barely touched the bike (ended up on an e-bike because I couldn’t face the real thing).

Then August 2024 happened.  
My wife sent a photo from our summer holiday — me getting out of the swimming pool. I looked in the mirror and thought: “I’m a fat bastard.” I hadn’t weighed myself or checked my BP all year because I knew it would worry me. But that photo was the final wake-up call. If I didn’t change direction, I’d be dead or facing serious liver issues by 50.

I started reading about addiction — how it’s really just dopamine hijacking your brain, and alcohol is one of the worst culprits.  
Once I understood that discomfort is normal (being a dad, husband, bloke in general means facing it), and that the craving was a chemical trick I could stare down, everything shifted. I didn’t need a miracle. Just some simple, consistent steps.

I quit for good.  
Started walking — a lot. No alcohol. Ate sensibly because I didn’t want to ruin the walks. Within seven months the weight fell off. Bought a pull-up bar, new Apple Watch, decent shoes, and started adding strength work at lunchtime (with tips from my brother). Tweaked around a pre-existing shoulder injury from mountain biking — pressed on with push-ups, chin-ups, weights when possible.

The results?  
Lost all the excess fat. Built lean mass. Blood pressure better than it’s ever been. Fitness age now 17–18. Energy endless. I’m up for anything — kids, wife, mates, adventures. I feel like my old self, but wiser and stronger.

Best bit?  
I didn’t have to stop living. Still see friends, still socialise — just without the drink. Everything continued; the poison stopped.

I’m stubborn as hell (ask my brothers and sister — I don’t take advice easily).  
But that stubbornness helped me test everything on myself first. I built a simple system: measure steps, track food (MyFitnessPal), lift progressively, face the discomfort head-on, adjust as you go. Blisters, sore muscles, aching knees — it all happened. But the rest fell into place.

I work in technology, so I love systems, tracking, and iterating.  
When I saw how transformative the other side is — and how straightforward the steps really are once you stop overcomplicating it — I realised I could help other men in the exact same boat. Blokes who’ve “failed before,” who think it’s too late, who blame themselves instead of the broken system. Men who want their energy back, their confidence, their hobbies, their health — without becoming a gym bro or giving up life (just the pints if they’re holding you back).

That’s why **Battleship – Midlife Fitness Reset** exists.  
Not as a quick fix or plaster. But as a proper rewrite: simple steps, accountability, real tracking, adjustments along the way. So you don’t become another statistic of an early death or a miserable midlife. You get to show up as the dad, husband, mate, and bloke you actually want to be — with energy, strength, and zero regrets.

And yes — the rusty camper van is getting used again. Properly.
