# Step-Up

## What it is
You step up onto a raised surface (a sturdy chair, step, or box) one foot at a time, stand tall at the top, then step back down. Simple as it sounds.

## Why it's in your plan
Step-ups train each leg independently — which means imbalances get corrected and your stronger leg can't compensate for your weaker one. They build glute and quad strength without loading your spine (unlike a barbell squat) and without the knee stress of lunges. For men with bad knees, step-ups are the single best lower body exercise — controlled range of motion, no impact, and you set the difficulty by the height of the step.

## How to do it
1. Stand facing a chair or step (roughly knee height, or lower if your knees are sensitive)
2. Place your right foot flat on the surface. Drive through that heel to stand up — bring your left foot up to meet it at the top
3. Step back down with the left foot first, then the right. That's one rep. Complete all reps on one side, then switch.

## What it should feel like
The working leg (the one on the step) should be doing all the work. Your glute and quad should feel the effort. If you're pushing off with your back foot on the floor, the step is too high — lower it.

## Adjusting difficulty
- Too easy: hold a dumbbell in each hand
- Too hard: use a lower step (even a thick book to start)
- Knee pain: lower the step height until pain-free. A 10cm step is still useful.

## Bad knee modification
This IS the knee modification — use this instead of lunges and Bulgarian split squats throughout the programme.
