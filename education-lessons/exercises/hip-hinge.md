# Hip Hinge (Dumbbell Romanian Deadlift)

## What it is
You push your hips backward while keeping your back flat, lowering dumbbells down the front of your legs — then drive your hips forward to stand back up. It looks like a bow. This is the foundation movement for all deadlift variations.

## Why it's in your plan
The hip hinge is the most important movement pattern for men over 40 with desk jobs. Sitting all day shortens your hip flexors and switches off your glutes — your body's largest and most powerful muscles. A switched-off backside means your lower back compensates for everything, which is why so many desk workers have chronic lower back pain. Hip hinges reactivate your glutes and hamstrings, take load off your spine, and build the posterior chain strength that makes everything from lifting shopping to getting off the sofa easier.

## How to do it
1. Stand holding a dumbbell in each hand, arms hanging in front of your thighs. Soft bend in the knees — not locked, not squatting.
2. Push your hips BACKWARD (imagine someone has a rope around your hips pulling them toward the wall behind you). The dumbbells slide down the front of your legs. Your back stays flat — not rounded. You'll feel a strong stretch in your hamstrings.
3. When you feel the stretch (roughly shin height for most people), drive your hips forward to return to standing. Squeeze your glutes at the top.

## What it should feel like
A deep stretch in the back of your thighs (hamstrings) on the way down. Your glutes contracting strongly on the way up. This is NOT a back exercise — if your lower back is working hard, you're bending your spine instead of hinging at the hips.

## The key cue
"Push hips back, not down." This is a hinge, not a squat. Your knees barely move — your hips do all the travel.

## Bad back modification
This is actually the safe version for bad backs — it keeps the spine neutral throughout. Start with very light weight (or no weight) until the movement pattern feels natural. Never round your lower back.
