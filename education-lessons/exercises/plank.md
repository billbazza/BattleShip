# Plank

## What it is
You hold a push-up position (or on your forearms) without moving — supporting your body in a straight line. A static hold, not a repetition exercise.

## Why it's in your plan
Your core isn't just your abs — it's the entire cylinder of muscles around your midsection including your lower back, obliques, and deep stabilisers. A strong core protects your spine during every other exercise and in daily life (picking things up, sitting at a desk, carrying bags). The plank trains all of these simultaneously without any spinal flexion (the crunching motion that aggravates back problems).

## How to do it
1. Get into a push-up position — hands directly under shoulders, body in a straight line from head to heels. Or drop to your forearms if wrists are uncomfortable.
2. Squeeze everything: glutes tight, stomach braced like you're about to take a punch, thighs engaged.
3. Hold. Breathe normally. Don't hold your breath.

## What it should feel like
Effort across your whole midsection. If you only feel it in your shoulders, your hips are too high. If you feel it in your lower back, your hips are too low — raise them slightly and squeeze your glutes harder.

## Durations to aim for
- Week 3: 3 × 15–20 seconds
- Week 6: 3 × 30 seconds
- Week 10: 3 × 45–60 seconds

Quality over duration. 20 seconds with perfect form beats 60 seconds with sagging hips.

## Bad back modification
If planks cause lower back pain, substitute bird-dog instead: on hands and knees, extend opposite arm and leg simultaneously, hold 3 seconds, repeat. Same core benefit, zero spinal compression.
