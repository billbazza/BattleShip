# Dumbbell Row

## What it is
You support yourself with one hand on a bench or chair, and pull a dumbbell upward with the other arm — like starting a lawnmower in slow motion. One side at a time.

## Why it's in your plan
Most men over 40 who sit at a desk have weak upper backs and tight chests — the classic rounded-shoulder posture. Rows directly fix this. They train the muscles between your shoulder blades (rhomboids, lats, rear deltoids) that desk work slowly switches off. Stronger back = better posture, less back pain, and a broader, more upright appearance. They also balance the push-ups — for every pushing movement, you need a pulling movement or your shoulders suffer.

## How to do it
1. Place your left hand and left knee on a bench or sturdy chair. Your back should be flat — parallel to the floor, not rounded.
2. Hold a dumbbell in your right hand, arm hanging straight down toward the floor.
3. Pull the dumbbell upward toward your hip (not your shoulder) — like you're starting a lawnmower. Squeeze your shoulder blade in at the top. Lower slowly. Complete all reps, then switch sides.

## What it should feel like
Effort in the middle of your back and the back of your shoulder. Your elbow drives upward and back — not outward. If you feel it mainly in your bicep, you're pulling with your arm rather than your back. Think "elbow to hip" not "hand to shoulder."

## If it feels awkward
Use a lighter weight and focus on feeling your shoulder blade move. The movement comes from your back, not your arm. It takes a few sessions to feel it correctly — that's normal.

## Bad back modification
This exercise is generally safe for lower back issues as your spine is supported. Keep your back flat throughout — don't rotate your torso to get the weight higher.
