# Sleep — The Easy Win Nobody Takes Seriously

## Bad sleep makes everything else harder

One bad night of sleep increases hunger hormones (ghrelin) by up to 24% the next day. Cortisol rises, insulin sensitivity drops, and your body preferentially stores fat rather than burning it. You make worse food decisions, skip the workout, and consume roughly 300–500 more calories than you would have on a good night.

Fix sleep and you get fat loss, better training, better mood, and sharper thinking — for free. No supplement, no diet change, nothing to buy. It's the highest-return investment in this entire programme and most people treat it as optional.

## The environment (the easy wins)

**Dark.**
Your brain produces melatonin — the hormone that triggers sleep — in response to darkness. Any light in your bedroom delays and reduces this process. Blackout blinds or an eye mask are not luxuries. They're tools. A good eye mask costs £10 and is one of the highest-return sleep investments you can make. Everyone knows the feeling of a cool, dark room — it's not a coincidence that hotels spend money on blackout curtains.

**Cold.**
Core body temperature needs to drop by about 1°C to initiate deep sleep. A cool room (around 16–19°C) facilitates this. If you're waking up hot in the night, your room is too warm. Keep it cooler than you think you need. The cold pillow — flipping it to the cool side — isn't just comfort, it's biology.

**Quiet.**
If noise is unavoidable (partner, street, thin walls), earplugs are a complete solution and cost less than a coffee. Silicone or foam — try both and find what's comfortable. White noise (a fan, a brown noise app) also works by masking irregular sounds rather than eliminating them.

## The routine (what you do before bed)

**No food 3–4 hours before sleep.**
Digestion raises core temperature and keeps your metabolism active. Eating late doesn't just risk extra calories — it directly disrupts the temperature drop your body needs to enter deep sleep.

**No alcohol within 2 hours (ideally more).**
Alcohol makes you fall asleep faster and wrecks sleep quality. It suppresses REM sleep, increases sleep fragmentation, and leaves you in a lighter, less restorative state all night. You wake up having "slept" but not having recovered. This is why a few drinks on a Tuesday make Wednesday feel difficult regardless of how many hours you were in bed.

**Shower before bed.**
A warm shower or bath 1–2 hours before bed causes your body to shed heat rapidly afterwards — accelerating the core temperature drop that triggers sleep. This is well-supported by sleep research and consistently underrated as a practical intervention.

**Dim the lights from 9pm.**
Bright overhead lights and screens emit blue light that suppresses melatonin. Dimmer, warmer light in the hour before bed signals to your brain that the day is ending. Blue light filter on your phone helps — but physical dimming of your environment works better.

## Meditation and wind-down

A racing mind is one of the most common reasons men over 40 struggle to fall asleep. The day doesn't stop just because you're lying down.

**What works:**

- **Body scan meditation** — systematically relaxing each muscle group from feet to head. Free on apps like Insight Timer, Calm, or Headspace. Takes 10–15 minutes and is genuinely effective.
- **Breathing** — 4-7-8 breathing (inhale for 4 counts, hold for 7, exhale for 8) activates the parasympathetic nervous system. It feels slightly ridiculous the first time. It works.
- **A written brain dump** — 5 minutes writing down everything on your mind before you try to sleep. Getting it onto paper removes the mental loop of "don't forget X".
- **Non-fiction books** — reading (not scrolling) before bed is one of the oldest and most reliable wind-down tools. Avoid thrillers if you're using it to wind down.

**What doesn't work:**
Scrolling. Regardless of what you're reading, the combination of blue light, infinite scroll, and emotionally activating content (news, social media) is physiologically opposed to sleep. Put the phone on the other side of the room.

## The one metric to track

How do you feel when you wake up — not how long you slept?

Eight hours of fragmented, light sleep leaves you feeling worse than six hours of deep, uninterrupted sleep. Duration matters, but quality matters more. If you wake up feeling unrefreshed after 7–8 hours, the issue is quality, not quantity — and the environmental fixes above address it directly.

## Bonus: Stress — the other thing blocking your results

Stress and sleep are the same problem from different directions. Both elevate cortisol — the hormone that tells your body to hold onto fat, particularly around the abdomen.

Cortisol is not the enemy. In short bursts it's essential — it's what gets you out of a burning building. The problem is chronic cortisol: the low-level, persistent elevation that comes from a demanding job, a full household, financial pressure, and not enough recovery. This is the background noise of most men's lives over 40. And it is directly, measurably antagonistic to fat loss and muscle building.

**What chronic stress does:**
- Raises blood sugar (your body preparing for a threat that never comes)
- Increases fat storage, particularly visceral abdominal fat
- Suppresses testosterone
- Disrupts sleep (which raises cortisol further — a loop)
- Increases cravings for high-calorie food (your brain seeking quick energy)

**The interventions that actually work:**

**Zone 2 walking is already handling this.** This is not a coincidence. Steady-state aerobic exercise is one of the most well-researched cortisol-lowering interventions available. If you've been doing your walks, you've been managing stress whether you thought about it that way or not.

**Cold water on the face.** Splashing cold water on your face or a brief cold shower activates the diving reflex — a hard-wired parasympathetic response that drops heart rate and cortisol rapidly. Takes 30 seconds. Works immediately.

**Breathing before you need it.** Slow, controlled exhales activate the vagus nerve and shift your nervous system from sympathetic (fight/flight) to parasympathetic (rest/digest). Five slow breaths — 4 seconds in, 6 seconds out — before a difficult meeting, after a hard commute, or before bed.

**Protect your sleep.** Everything in this lesson. Sleep is not a stress management technique — it is the stress management technique. Everything else is supplementary.

**One thing you can actually control.** Stress itself is often not controllable. Your response to it is. Identify the one recurring stressor in your week that you have some influence over — a morning routine, an inbox that never empties, a commitment you could decline — and address just that one thing. Don't try to solve all of it. Solve one piece.
