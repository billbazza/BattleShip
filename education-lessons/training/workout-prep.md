# Workout Prep

## The session starts before you pick up a weight

Most people walk into the gym, go straight to a machine, and wonder why they feel stiff and weak for the first 10 minutes. Or worse, they pull something. Preparation takes 10 minutes and determines the quality of the 40 minutes that follow.

## The night before

**Pack your bag.** Obvious, but the number one reason people skip morning sessions is standing in the kitchen at 6am trying to find their trainers. Remove every friction point. Bag packed, clothes laid out, water bottle filled.

**Eat something.** You don't need a full meal before training — but training completely fasted isn't ideal for performance or muscle preservation. A banana, a small bowl of oats, or some Greek yoghurt 30–60 minutes before is enough.

**Sleep.** More on this in a later lesson, but: a bad night's sleep produces a bad session. If you had a rough night, adjust your expectations — go in and move, but don't chase personal bests.

## The warm-up (do this every session)

**5 minutes of low-intensity movement** — a brisk walk on the treadmill, a slow 5 minutes on a bike, or light rowing. Not to get sweaty — just to raise your core temperature and get blood moving.

**Then: movement prep.** Specific to what you're training.

For a lower body session:
- 10 bodyweight squats (slow, full range)
- 10 hip hinges (no weight, just the pattern)
- 10 hip circles each leg
- 10 glute bridges

For an upper body session:
- 10 arm circles (forward and back)
- 10 band pull-aparts or light face-pulls
- 10 shoulder rotations
- 5 press-ups (slowly)

**Warm-up sets.** Before your first working set of any exercise, do one set at roughly half your working weight. This primes the specific muscles and movement pattern. It's not wasted effort — it makes every working set better.

## Between sets

Rest properly. 60–90 seconds for most exercises. Up to 2 minutes for heavier compound lifts. Use the time — have some water, check your notes, don't stare at your phone for 4 minutes.

## After the session

Two minutes of light stretching — specifically the muscles you worked. Hold each stretch for 20–30 seconds. This won't make or break your progress, but it helps recovery and is a useful signal to your nervous system that the session is over.

## The mindset going in

You're not trying to destroy yourself. You're applying a controlled, progressive stimulus and then leaving. Sessions that leave you unable to walk for three days aren't productive — they're ego. Get in, do the work, get out. Consistency over intensity.
