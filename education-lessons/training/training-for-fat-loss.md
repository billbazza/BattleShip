# Training for Fat Loss

## Why lifting weights beats cardio for body composition

Cardio burns calories while you're doing it. A 45-minute run burns roughly 400–600 calories — and then stops. Strength training burns slightly fewer calories during the session — but continues burning calories for 24–48 hours afterwards as your muscles repair. And over months, as muscle mass increases, your resting metabolic rate goes up permanently.

More muscle = more calories burned at rest. Every day. Without doing anything.

A man with 5kg more muscle than another man of the same weight burns roughly 250–350 more calories per day just existing. That's not nothing — over a year, that's the equivalent of losing an additional 10–12kg of fat from doing nothing different.

## The combination that actually works

Walking (Zone 2 cardio) + strength training + a slight calorie deficit is the most effective body composition approach for men over 40. Not one of these alone — all three working together.

- **Walking** builds the aerobic base, burns fat during the activity, improves insulin sensitivity
- **Strength training** builds muscle, raises metabolism, preserves lean mass during a deficit
- **Calorie deficit** ensures the body is mobilising stored fat for fuel

Remove any one of the three and the other two are less effective.

## What strength training does that cardio doesn't

**Preserves muscle during fat loss.**
When you're in a calorie deficit, your body will use both fat and muscle for energy if you give it the chance. Strength training signals that muscle is needed — your body preferentially burns fat instead. Without it, up to 40% of weight lost in a deficit comes from muscle. With it, that number drops dramatically.

**Changes shape, not just size.**
Losing fat through cardio and diet alone makes you smaller. Losing fat while building muscle changes your shape — broader shoulders, defined arms, flatter stomach — even at the same weight. This is what "toning" actually means, and the only way to achieve it is resistance training.

**Improves everything else.**
Bone density (critical for men over 40 — osteoporosis isn't just a women's issue), posture, joint health, testosterone levels, sleep quality, cognitive function. The research on resistance training and longevity is overwhelming. This is not optional self-improvement — it's maintenance.

## Sets, reps, and fat loss

For fat loss specifically, the rep range doesn't matter as much as people think. What matters is: progressive overload, sufficient weekly volume (roughly 10–20 sets per muscle group per week), and adequate protein to preserve muscle.

You don't need to do "high reps for tone" or "low reps for bulk." That's a myth. Heavy and light both work if volume and progressive overload are present.

## How long before you notice

**Week 2–3:** You'll feel stronger. The same weights feel easier.
**Week 4–6:** Clothes start to fit differently even if the scale hasn't moved much.
**Week 8–12:** Visible changes. Other people notice.

The scale is a poor judge of progress during the early months of strength training. Muscle is being built while fat is being lost — these changes can roughly cancel each other out on a scale while transforming how you look. Measure your waist, take photos, track how your clothes fit.
