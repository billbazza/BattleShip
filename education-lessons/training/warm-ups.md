# Proper Warm-Ups

## Why most people skip it and why that's a mistake

The warm-up feels like a tax on the session. You're already pressed for time. Getting straight into the working sets seems more efficient.

It isn't. A cold muscle is a stiff muscle. A stiff muscle produces less force and absorbs impact less effectively. Skipping the warm-up for a man over 40 is how you turn a productive training session into three weeks of physiotherapy.

Your connective tissue — tendons and ligaments — has a worse blood supply than muscle. It warms up more slowly and is more vulnerable to injury when cold. After 40, this matters more than it did at 25. The warm-up isn't optional. It's 10 minutes that protects the other 35.

## The structure

**Part 1: Raise core temperature (3–5 minutes)**
Light cardio. Brisk walk, slow jog, cycling, rowing — any of these. The goal is to raise your body temperature slightly and increase blood flow. You should feel slightly warm but not breathless.

**Part 2: Mobility work for the joints you're about to use (3–5 minutes)**

*For lower body sessions:*
- Hip circles: stand on one foot, draw large circles with the opposite knee. 10 each direction, each side.
- Bodyweight squat: 10 slow reps, focusing on depth and control
- Hip hinge: 10 reps with no weight, pushing hips back deliberately
- Glute bridge: lying on your back, feet flat, drive hips up. 15 reps.

*For upper body sessions:*
- Arm circles: 10 forward, 10 backward
- Shoulder rotations: hands on shoulders, rotate elbows in large circles
- Band pull-aparts or light face-pulls: 15 reps (if bands available)
- Slow press-ups: 10 reps, 3-second lowering phase

**Part 3: Warm-up sets (before each exercise)**
Before your first working set, do one set at approximately half your working weight for 10–15 easy reps. This rehearses the specific movement pattern at low intensity before the demand goes up.

If you're squatting 50kg, do a set with the bar (20kg) first. If you're pressing 20kg dumbbells, start with 10kg. This is not wasted effort — it's calibration.

## The difference between warm-up soreness and pain

As you move in the warm-up, you may feel some stiffness or general muscle soreness from previous sessions. This is fine and will ease as you warm up.

**Stop if you feel:**
- Sharp or acute pain in a specific joint
- Pain that worsens as you move rather than eases
- Any clicking or catching sensation in a joint that feels wrong rather than just tight

Dull ache in muscles from previous sessions = proceed. Sharp or worsening joint pain = stop and investigate.

## One useful habit

Rate your warm-up on a scale of 1–10 for how your body feels. Write it down. Over time, you'll notice patterns: you move better on days you slept well, after rest days, when you've eaten properly. This data is more useful than any fitness tracker.
