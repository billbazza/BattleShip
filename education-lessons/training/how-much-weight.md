# How Much Weight?

## The question every beginner asks and nobody answers directly

The answer: **start lighter than you think you need to, and add weight every week.**

That's it. But let's make it practical.

## Finding your starting weight

For any new exercise, do this:

1. Pick a weight that feels easy for 10 reps
2. Do 15 reps — it should feel manageable but not trivial by rep 15
3. If it felt easy at 15: that's your warm-up weight, go heavier
4. If you could only get to 10–12 before form broke down: you're close to the right zone
5. Rest and try the next weight up

You're looking for a weight where **reps 8–10 feel genuinely effortful but doable with good form.** The last two reps of a set should require focus. The first two shouldn't require much effort at all.

## The rep ranges to know

**8–12 reps per set:** The standard range for muscle building (hypertrophy). Most of your programme will live here.

**12–15 reps per set:** Lighter weight, higher reps. Good for learning the movement pattern, lower injury risk, still builds muscle. A sensible starting range if you're new or returning from injury.

**5–8 reps per set:** Heavier weight, fewer reps. More strength-focused. Less appropriate for beginners — technique needs to be solid first.

For your programme, start in the 12–15 range. Once the pattern feels natural (2–3 weeks), shift to 8–12.

## The rule of progressive overload in practice

When you can complete the **top of your rep range for all sets with good form**, add weight at the next session.

Example: You're doing goblet squats, 3 sets of 8–12 reps, at 16kg.
- Week 1: 10, 9, 8 reps ← not quite at the top, stay at 16kg
- Week 2: 12, 11, 10 reps ← getting there
- Week 3: 12, 12, 12 reps ← top of range, all sets ← **add weight next session**
- Week 4: 8, 8, 7 reps at 18kg ← back to the bottom of the range, repeat

This is the entire system. There is no secret beyond this.

## Ego and the gym

Men have a particular tendency to pick weights that are too heavy and compensate with terrible form. The result is half the muscle stimulus and double the injury risk.

The man in the corner doing dumbbell rows with 40kg and swinging his entire body is getting less back development than the man next to him doing 20kg with a controlled, deliberate pull. Form is not optional. It's the mechanism.

If someone in the gym is lifting more than you — so what. They've either been at it longer, or they're doing it wrong. Either way, it has nothing to do with your session.

## A practical starting point for common exercises

These are ballpark figures for men over 40 returning to training. Adjust based on how you feel:

| Exercise | Starting weight |
|----------|----------------|
| Goblet squat | 8–12kg dumbbell |
| Dumbbell Romanian deadlift | 10–16kg each hand |
| Dumbbell row | 10–16kg |
| Dumbbell press (chest) | 8–12kg each hand |
| Overhead press | 6–10kg each hand |
| Step-up | Bodyweight first, then add dumbbells |
| Plank | Bodyweight, time-based |
