# The Battleship Training Method

## The most common mistake in the gym

Changing your workout every week.

Instagram, YouTube, and the fitness industry have a financial incentive to keep you buying new programmes, trying new exercises, and believing that "muscle confusion" is a real thing. It isn't. Muscles don't get confused. They adapt — and then require progressive overload to keep adapting. You can't progressively overload an exercise you've never done twice.

The man who has squatted consistently for 12 weeks with steadily increasing weight is stronger and has more muscle than the man who tried squats for two weeks, switched to leg press, switched to leg extensions, then went back to squats. The first man's body knows what's coming and has adapted to it. The second man's body never got the signal clearly enough to respond.

**Consistency at the same exercises, over time, with more weight = the entire method.**

## Stick to the same routine for 8–12 weeks

This feels wrong because it feels boring. It feels like you're not doing enough, not varying enough, not working as hard as the person doing a different workout every day.

But boredom in the gym is actually the signal that your nervous system has learned the movement efficiently. That's when the real muscular adaptation begins. The awkward, uncertain phase of a new exercise is your nervous system figuring out motor patterns — you're not getting much hypertrophy yet. Once the movement feels natural, that's when the muscle-building stimulus truly kicks in.

Stay the course for 8 weeks minimum. We'll tell you when it's time to change.

## The exercises that matter most

Phase 1 is built around compound movements — exercises that work multiple muscle groups simultaneously. Not because isolation exercises are bad, but because compound movements give you the most return on your time.

**The big movements:**
- **Squat pattern** — goblet squat, front squat, barbell squat
- **Hip hinge pattern** — Romanian deadlift, conventional deadlift
- **Horizontal push** — dumbbell press, push-up, bench press
- **Horizontal pull** — dumbbell row, cable row, machine row
- **Vertical push** — overhead press
- **Vertical pull** — lat pulldown, assisted pull-up, pull-up

Six movement patterns. Everything else is accessory work. Master these first and you'll have developed the majority of the muscle that's possible to develop.

## What about arms?

They'll develop. A dumbbell row works your biceps significantly. A press works your triceps significantly. By the time you've done 8 weeks of compound training, your arms will be noticeably stronger and more defined — without a single curl.

In phase 2 we add some direct arm work. Not because it's the priority — but because by then the foundation is solid and the addition makes sense. Adding isolation exercises before you've mastered the compounds is like learning to play guitar solos before you can play chords. Get the fundamentals right first.

## Why we won't tell you to "mix it up"

Every fitness platform has an incentive to sell you new content. Variety is content. New exercises, new challenges, new routines — these are products.

Progress is not a product. It's the result of doing the same things correctly for longer than feels necessary. We're not going to tell you to switch things up every two weeks because it would make for engaging content. We're going to tell you to stay on your programme until it's genuinely time to progress — because that's what works.
